10/14/2020 - Research Questions

1. What is the correlation between location (of both the accused and site of the trial) and severity of punishment sentenced to the accused?

2. What is the connection between the time of year and the number of accused witches? Possibly showing an influence of weather conditions on attitudes towards accused witches.

3. Is the “Witch Gene” passed between immediate family members? If one member of the family is accused does that mean other relatives are likely to be seen as witches as well?

4. Is there any prejudice towards someone being a witch based on their ethnic background?

5. Did gender play a significant role in who was accused and why might this be?

6. Did marital status play a significant role in who was accused and does it correlate to social status?

7. Did past crimes or convictions contribute to accusations of being a witch?

8. Was the location within the town significant in who was accused?

9. How did conflict (ie. wars, revolution, famine) in Scotland affect the rate of witch accusations?

10. How did conflict (ie. wars, revolution, famine) in Scotland affect the ration acquitted to those punished?

11. How did the publishing of witchcraft accounts affect the number of accused witches?

12. How did the publishing of witchcraft accounts affect the characterization of accusations?

13. How did a spike of witch accusations in a given area of Scotland affect rates of accusations in other areas?

14. What is the impact/correlation of different demographics (gender, marital status, etc) affect the type of witchcraft they were accused of?

15. How did scientific and medical advancements in Europe in the 16th and 17th centuries affect the rate of witchcraft accusations, and what witches were being accused for?

10/7/2020 - What is Digital Humanities?

1. Digital Humanities is at the intersection of traditional humanities subjects and modern computational methods.
According to Burdick et al, there is a significant “disconnect” between traditional methods of studying humanities “inherited from cloisters and seminar rooms” and our “massively mediated culture” (24). The emergence of digital humanities has begun to rebuild that connection, combining aspects of both traditional and modern methods.

2. Digital Humanities is an extension of traditional knowledge skills and methods, not a replacement for them.
Digital Humanities does not rely solely on the use of digital tools and engagement, but it takes advantage of these tools to explore traditional concepts in new ways. In Digital_Humanities, Burdick explains, “Its distinctive contributions do not obliterate the insights of the past, but add and supplement the humanities’ long-standing commitment to scholarly interpretation, informed research, structured argument, and dialogue within communities of practice” (16). For example, in Data Points, Nathan Yau mentions Santiago Ortiz’s “View of the Sky,” which visualizes the stars in several different ways (36). Some of these visualizations are similar to a classic astronomy map that can be seen in many traditional classrooms. In other visualizations, however, Ortiz uses digital tools and manipulation to give new perspective and meaning to the same data.

3. Digital humanities projects are inherently collaborative and generative.
Whereas traditional humanities research relies on individual achievements, Burdick et al. argues that “digital projects of any substantial scale benefit from and, indeed, often require team-based approaches” (15). A shift toward collaboration could bring much-needed diversity of voices and perspectives to the academic world, allowing for the exploration of new ideas and innovative ways of portraying them.

4. Digital humanities expands the study of the humanities by offering more ways for it to display data.
Since its inception, the humanities have relied on writing and text to display data (Burdick 7). This reliance has limited the interpretations that the field can produce, as the method of display itself can influence how the data is interpreted (Yau 6). The digital humanities offer various tools and methods to visually display data that allows researchers to explore the humanities’ data in new and interesting ways, creating a more nuanced investigation of the data that can benefit the field of humanities as a whole.

5. Digital data visualization offers new ways to look at human history.
Trouillot observes that humans are most captivated by the large, material markers of history such as mass graves and pyramids, which “bring history closer while they make us feel small” and “seem to speak of an immensity of which we know little except that we are part of it” (29-30). Digital humanities offers a counter to this immensity through analysis and visualization of data. The balance of detailed data analysis alongside these captivating material objects creates a more nuanced way to look at the past.

6. Digital Humanities expands the humanities by creating new forms of collaboration.
In Digital_Humanities, Burdick highlights the multitude of collaborative mediums that the study of digital humanities curates, where there is “ample room for specialization and, particularly, for collaboration” because digital humanities projects “often require team-based approaches” (15). A worldwide platform for dialogue provided by digital tools, expanding humanities into the hands of others, when before this would never have been available without technological advances.

7. The humanities offer a rich selection of contextualized data that statisticians and other interested parties can explore in the digital humanities.
Thorough evaluation of data requires clear contextualization. As statistician and data visualization expert, Nathan Yau states, contextualization “can completely change your perspective on a dataset, and it can help you decide what the numbers represent and how to interpret them.” (Yau 35). Luckily for statisticians and others who work in the digital realm, the humanities have created an extensive reservoir of contextualized data ready to be analyzed.

8. Digital Humanities invites a wider audience into specified fields through the use of meaningful visualizations.
In his work Data Points, Nathan Yau writes, “Analysis and storytelling are one in the same” (15). Good visualization helps portray what would otherwise be an overwhelming amount of raw data for people to comprehend, using both statistics and design skills to articulate the data into a cohesive story. “Without the former, the visualization becomes an exercise only in illustration and aesthetics, and without the latter, one of only analyses” (Yau xi). For example, Aaron Parecki, a Portland-based developer, used 2.5 million GPS points of his daily whereabouts to create a personal atlas (Yau 8), presenting the audience with both implied meaning and personalized interpretation of his massive dataset.

9. Digital humanities highlight social inequalities.
The field of digital humanities is an excellent field for public projects that highlight inequalities in society. Projects like Public Secret and Million Dollar Hoods both focus on the problems of mass incarceration in California. The researcher of Public Secret, Sharron Daniel, sees her project as an abolitionist project “that aims to dismantle the prison system in pursuit of a more humane world.” Similarly, Million Dollar Hoods states that its goal is “to end mass incarceration” (Lytle). The publication of these projects on accessible websites allows for the information displayed to inform the general public.

10. Digital Humanities offers a potential renewal for “humanistic scholarship.”
In Digital_Humanities, Burdick et al explain how their book seeks to “demonstrate the contributions of contemporary humanities scholarship to new modes of knowledge formation enabled by networked, digital environments” (7). While the humanities are often considered to be in “crisis,” investment in digital humanities can counter the field’s demise. Digital humanities positions itself to combine traditional long-form written text with new digital methods. This has the potential to “save” the humanities, by expanding the common definition of the humanities itself.

11. Digital Humanities addresses the increasingly varied forms in which we communicate.
As Burdick et al points out, “in the 21st century, we communicate in media significantly more varied, extensible, and multiplicative than linear text” (10). This varied media might include blogs, videos, social media, and more. No longer is linear text the only viable way to look at the humanities. Digital humanities looks to remedy this by providing new ways to analyze and look at the work as it evolves and changes.

12. Digital Humanities helps address the increasingly globalized world we live in.
As the world becomes more globalized and interconnected, the western views that have shaped much of the humanities become increasingly outdated. When referencing comparisons of European language with differing types of language in “Silencing the Past,” Trouillot explains, “we now know that both sides were wrong; grammar functions in all languages. Could the same be said about history...?” (7). Digital humanities opens the door for more broad and globalized views, creating new ways to question and analyze language and history.

10/7/2020 - Data Critique

The Survey of Scottish Witchcraft is a database containing information about all known individuals accused of witchcraft in early modern Scotland. The database was published in 2003 by Julian Goodare, Lauren Martin, Joyce Miller, and Lousie Yeoman. They intended to “collect collate and record all known information about accused witches and witchcraft belief in a Microsoft Access database and to create a web-based user interface for the database.” The data was collected by first looking at existing surveys of Scottish witchcraft then by archival research over two years by the two full-time researchers, Joyce Miller and Lauren Martin. Charlotte Moon also created a website and database tools for the project. The project director was Dr. Julian Goodare, a professor of Scottish history at the University of Edinburgh. The co-director was Dr. Louise Yeoman, a former curator at the National Library of Scotland and researcher with BBC Scotland.

The Survey of Scottish Witchcraft used three previous surveys to jump-start the research for their own database. They used George Black’s 1938 Calendar of Cases of Witchcraft in Scotland: 1510-1727. The SSW used this non-comprehensive survey because it lists close to 1,000 specific trials, and because it includes lists of cases and local published sources. The team did acknowledge that the source was limited because Black did not work with any manuscripts that could have contained more information. The next survey, Source-Book of Scottish Witchcraft, was researched and written by Christia Larner, Christopher Hyde Lee, and Hugh McLachlan, and published in 1977. This survey used manuscripts and had information on 3,000 cases. This survey would be revised by Stuart Macdonald in the 1990s. From this work, McLachlan created the Scottish Witch Hunt Data Base, and from this database the SSW added 1,000 more cases and expanded the information about each case.

From here, the SSW was expanded from the use of various sources such as Private Papers belonging to the Maxwell of Pollok family, the Burgh Court Books, and Burgh Treasurer’s Accounts, assumed information to specific cases. Miscellaneous manuscript sources were also referenced, such as the Aberdeen City Archives, a box containing items relating to the witchcraft trials from Aberdeen between 1595 and 1597, mostly including paperwork records, as well as printed records of the parliamentary meetings and decisions of Parliament of Scotland. The Miscellany of the Spalding Club Volumes I, IV and V, held edited transcripts of documents from witchcraft trials. These were held in Aberdeen in the 16th century and provided specific case information. The data gathered includes any type of information on trials, lore, investigations, manuals; anything related to witchcraft in Scotland. Examples of this can be seen in all of the sources used to compile this data. Orkney Folklore and Traditions informed the research team of folklore traditions in the Orkney Islands, which also included accusations and specific prosecutions. Witchcraft trials were recorded in edited transcripts from Spottiswoode Miscellany: a collection of original papers and tracts, illustrative chiefly of the civil and ecclesiastical history of Scotland, volume 2. They acquired an edited transcript of the Campbells and hereditary jurisdiction from The Justiciary Records of Argyll and the Isles, volume 1. However, much like many of the references, it is not a complete record of the proceedings, and the source of compilation is unknown, meaning it was possibly taken from one or more records. The reason for belief is due to no official authenticated signatures, but the information from the chosen records was still recorded and categorized to be included in this data set.

The Survey of Scottish Witchcraft built a web version of the database, designed to help the public and academic researchers to explore the organized files in a digital setting. The database formatted in this way has allowed for structural patterns to be actualized. Providing a digital format for this data was a way to provide an examination on biographical and social information about the accused witches—such as age, sex, marital status, or economic class—and even draw information on what the town was like for the accused. Information like this also falls under a cultural category, like belief systems and legal proceedings of the trials. The interactive maps give insight into national and regional variations, and the chronological and geographical placements of both the accused and prosecutors.

The raw data itself is quite extensive, including name, surname, maiden name, sex, settlement, country, etc. One of the shortcomings, however, is in the apparent lack of data for some entries. Almost every entry includes name, surname, sex, and country, but many are missing data for age, marital status, socioeconomic status, occupation, and other notes. This lack of consistent data may present some biases or limits when analyzing these data points.

Citation

Julian Goodare, Lauren Martin, Joyce Miller and Louise Yeoman, 'The Survey of Scottish Witchcraft', www.shc.ed.ac.uk/witches/ (archived January 2003, accessed October 2020).
